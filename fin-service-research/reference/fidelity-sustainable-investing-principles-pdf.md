## Overview

The Sustainable Investing Principles (SI Principles) document provides the foundational beliefs and overall approach underpinning Fidelity's Sustainable Investing Framework (FSIF). It details how Fidelity integrates sustainability into its investment process across all asset classes and geographies.

## Core Sustainable Investing Beliefs

### 1. ESG Integration is Fundamental to Long-Term Value

Fidelity believes that the integration of sustainability into investment research and portfolio construction is part of its fundamental process to identify the drivers of long-term value creation.

> "We believe that working with companies to improve their sustainability practices and awareness of ESG risks will help create more resilient business models, which should in turn translate into the long-term sustainability of a company and positive investor returns."

### 2. Double Materiality

Fidelity has evolved from a purely financial materiality lens (ESG 1.0) to a **double materiality** approach (ESG 2.0):

- **Outside-in (financial materiality):** How environmental and social factors affect a company's financial performance and risk profile
- **Inside-out (impact materiality):** How a company's activities impact people, society, and the planet

This dual lens recognises that companies are not only responsible for managing the financial risk of the social and environmental factors upon which they depend, but are also responsible for the actual impact that their business has on people and the planet.

### 3. Engagement Over Exclusion

Fidelity's fundamental investment philosophy is to **favour engagement over exclusion**. The firm believes that:

- If an investor sells, there is by definition a buyer — the seller is simply relinquishing their responsibility
- Engagement should play a vital role in sustainable investing to alter corporate behaviour
- By working closely with a company, Fidelity can determine how serious it is about its ethical responsibilities

Exclusions are applied only where certain economic activities give rise to unacceptable or unmanageable environmental or social risks that may be difficult or impossible for issuers to mitigate.

### 4. Proprietary ESG Ratings System (ESG 2.0)

Fidelity has developed a proprietary ESG ratings system covering approximately **4,000 issuers**, which:

- Makes holistic assessments of how companies manage their environmental impact and mitigate adverse effects on employees and society
- Encompasses corporate engagement and voting, thematic engagement campaigns, climate ratings, and contributions to the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
- Is more forward-looking than backward-looking data
- Moves from relative to absolute assessments of sustainability performance
- Covers smaller companies that might not have the capacity to invest in sustainability disclosure

The rationale for a proprietary system is the low correlation between ratings from external ESG providers, which shows that contrasting processes measure different things. Fidelity's approach places a great deal of emphasis on qualitative assessment — by talking to company management teams, customers, suppliers and competitors, it can make assessments that help its teams understand the direction of travel across industries.

### 5. Systemic Risk and Stewardship

Fidelity recognises that systemic risks (climate change, biodiversity loss, social disparities) require a multi-layered approach:

1. **System-wide influence:** Climate, net zero and exclusion policies
2. **Value chain understanding:** Fully comprehending a particular business and its dependencies
3. **Stock/asset level engagement:** Active corporate engagement supported by proprietary ESG ratings

This is what is often called **Systemic stewardship or Beta stewardship** — using investors' influence to address systemic problems through policy advocacy and engagement with the broader ecosystem.

## ESG Integration Approach

### Investment Research Integration

Fidelity's investment analysts are trained to integrate ESG factors into their fundamental research. The ESG ratings system provides:

- A structured framework for assessing material ESG risks and opportunities
- Forward-looking assessments based on direct company engagement
- Climate ratings aligned with net zero transition pathways
- SDG contribution mapping

### Stewardship Activities

Fidelity's stewardship approach uses multiple levers:

- **Voting:** At shareholder meetings globally, with clear guidelines on climate, governance, and social issues
- **Engagement:** Year-round dialogue with company management, boards, and policymakers
- **Collaborative engagement:** Participation in initiatives like Climate Action 100+, Finance for Biodiversity Pledge
- **Policy advocacy:** Engagement with regulators on SFDR, CSRD, and other sustainability regulations

### Climate Commitments

- Net zero across investment portfolios by **2050**
- Halving portfolio carbon footprint by **2030** (from baseline)
- Phase out thermal coal investment by **2030** (OECD) and **2040** (rest of world)
- Member of the Net Zero Asset Managers initiative

### Nature and Biodiversity

- Signatory of the Finance for Biodiversity Pledge (150+ institutions, $22 trillion+ AUM)
- Published Nature Roadmap outlining approach to nature integration in investments and stewardship
- Voting guidelines targeting companies with high deforestation risk

## Sustainable Investing Framework Structure

The SI Principles underpin the three-tier FSIF:

| Category | ESG Approach | Regulatory Alignment |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| ESG Unconstrained | May or may not integrate ESG | SFDR Article 6 |
| ESG Tilt | Promotes E&S characteristics via ESG tilt | SFDR Article 8 |
| ESG Target | ESG/sustainability as key objective | SFDR Article 8 & 9 |
